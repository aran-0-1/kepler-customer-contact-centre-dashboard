The analysis began by examining where customer interactions originated from, which channels were used, and the main reasons customers contacted Kepler. The data shows that 30% of Kepler’s interactions came from three states: California, Texas and Florida. In terms of channel usage, Call was the most common starting channel at 32%, followed by Chatbot at 25%, while the remaining interactions came through Email and Web.

A further finding from the dashboard was that the channel used by customers may be influenced by the reason for contact. For example, in this dataset, the contact reason Payments is linked entirely to the Call channel, while Service Outage is linked to 0% Call and relies fully on digital channels. However, the largest driver of contact volume was Billing Question, which accounted for nearly 71% of the total 32,941 interactions. This makes Billing Question a clear area for further investigation from a business analysis perspective, as even small improvements in this area could reduce a large share of overall contact demand.

The analysis then moved to response times and interaction volume during October 2020. Overall, the contact centre starts the week more slowly and becomes busier as the week progresses, with average interactions peaking on Friday before falling over the weekend. However, this pattern varies by location, and the overall differences between weekdays are relatively small. The least busy day receives 1,045 interactions, while the busiest day receives 1,170 interactions, giving a difference of 125 interactions. Although this difference may appear limited, the average interaction length is 25 minutes, meaning even a small increase in daily interaction volume could create meaningful staffing demand.

Response time performance appears strong at first glance, with 87% of interactions meeting Service Level Agreement (SLA) targets. However, this does not fully translate into positive customer experience, as 52% of interactions were classified as negative sentiment. This is also concerning alongside a 63% non-response rate for Customer Satisfaction (CSAT) Score, which limits the amount of satisfaction feedback available for analysis. Among customers who did provide a CSAT score, 65% gave a score of 6 or below. At a CSAT score of 6, sentiment remained mixed, with 56% negative and 44% neutral. Negative sentiment was only removed from CSAT score 7 onwards, where responses shifted towards a mix of neutral and positive sentiment. This suggests that meeting SLA targets alone may not be enough to create a positive customer experience, and Kepler should investigate the root causes behind negative sentiment in customer interactions.

The results were mainly consistent across different locations, channels and contact reasons. For example, selecting a specific location, channel or contact reason did not significantly alter the overall patterns in the dashboard. In a real business environment, greater variation would normally be expected. For instance, one channel might be linked to longer average interaction durations, a specific location might be more likely to miss SLA targets, or a particular contact reason might be associated with higher negative sentiment. The limited variation in these patterns is likely linked to the dataset being generated for practice purposes, where relationships between fields may be less complex than in real operational data.
